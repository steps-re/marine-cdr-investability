## Claim 1
**Claim:** CarbonRun received the world's first verified River Alkalinity Enhancement carbon credits (about 76.69 tonnes) from the registry Isometric in January 2026, for a project on the Kvina River in Norway, and has a Frontier offtake of 55,442 tonnes.

VERDICT: UNVERIFIABLE

The part of the claim regarding the Frontier offtake agreement for 55,442 tonnes is confirmed in separate reports.

## Claim 4
**Claim:** Across the marine/aquatic CDR industry, delivered/verified removal is only about 2 percent of contracted offtake volume.

VERDICT: PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED

CORRECTION: While the precise 2% figure is not explicitly confirmed in recent market reports, the claim's sentiment is accurate. The marine Carbon Dioxide Removal (mCDR) industry is nascent, and verified deliveries are a very small fraction of contracted volumes. According to an October 2025 market analysis, a total of 578,000 tonnes of CO2 removals have been secured through offtake agreements, but only 0.3% of these credits have been formally issued. The first verified mCDR credits were only delivered in 2025, highlighting the gap between long-term purchase agreements and current supply.

2. https://www.worthy.earth/writing/year-in-review-2025-marine-carbon-dioxide-removal-milestones

## Claim 7
**Claim:** Hurd et al. 2024 (Journal of Phycology) found that sinking Sargassum seaweed yields only about 6-33 percent of its nominal carbon value as durable atmospheric removal after accounting for air-sea equilibration.

VERDICT: PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED

CORRECTION: The paper is "Sinking Sargassum for carbon dioxide removal: The need to account for air-sea CO2 equilibration" by Hurd, Law, and Bach. It was published in the *Journal of Phycology* (Volume 60, Issue 1), which is dated 2024, though it was first available online in November 2023. While the paper argues that the net carbon removal is a fraction of the sunk carbon due to air-sea equilibration, the specific "6-33 percent" range could not be verified in publicly accessible abstracts or summaries. The core scientific claim about reduced efficiency is accurate.

## Claim 8
**Claim:** Offshore sub-seabed CO2 storage in the Baltic Sea is currently restricted/banned under the Helsinki Convention.

VERDICT: PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED

CORRECTION: The Helsinki Convention does not explicitly ban CO2 storage. However, the current, prevailing interpretation among most signatory parties is that sub-seabed CO2 storage is prohibited under the convention's general ban on dumping. This interpretation is being debated, with some parties like Poland arguing it is not prohibited in the subsoil. A formal legal review to clarify the matter is underway and expected by 2026.

## Claim 11
**Claim:** Chile's Pacific coastline is dominated by the Humboldt Current upwelling system, which is a net CO2-outgassing (source) region, making it unfavorable for atmosphere-coupled marine CDR.

VERDICT: PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED
CORRECTION: The Humboldt Current upwelling system is a region of significant CO2 outgassing because it brings CO2-rich deep water to the surface. However, it also stimulates immense biological productivity that absorbs CO2, creating a complex dynamic of sources and sinks. While some areas are confirmed CO2 sources, the net balance for the entire system is not definitively stated as a source in all conditions. The high background CO2 and natural outgassing do make it a challenging and potentially unfavorable location for marine CDR techniques like artificial upwelling, which could release more CO2 than is captured.
SOURCES:
1. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-021-94997-5 (Referenced in search results)
2. https://www.researchgate.net/publication/225320530_The_Humboldt_Current_System_Ecosystem_components_and_processes_fisheries_and_sediment_studies
3. https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK579823/


## Claim 12
**Claim:** New Zealand's Environmental Protection Authority rejected a Gigablue ocean carbon project on the grounds that it amounted to illegal dumping rather than scientific research.

VERDICT: PARTIALLY-CONFIRMED

CORRECTION: The claim accurately describes the reasoning for a project's rejection by New Zealand's Environmental Protection Authority (EPA), but it names the wrong company. The EPA has treated the activities of **Gigablue** as scientific research. The company whose project was rejected in May 2023 on the grounds that it constituted prohibited dumping rather than legitimate scientific research was the **Oceaneos Marine Research Foundation**. The EPA committee determined the primary purpose of the Oceaneos application was to prove a concept for commercial purposes, not scientific research as defined by law.
