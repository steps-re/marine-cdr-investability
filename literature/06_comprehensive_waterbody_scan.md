### Air-Water CO2 Equilibration in Halifax Harbour vs. the Open Ocean

The OAE project by **Planetary Technologies** in **Halifax Harbour, Nova Scotia, Canada** provides a specific case study for comparing air-water CO2 equilibration in a coastal embayment versus the open ocean.

Halifax Harbour is a fjord-like estuary with significant freshwater input, which creates a two-layered flow system and seasonal stratification. These characteristics lead to notable differences in CO2 equilibration compared to the open ocean. The surface waters of the harbor experience greater seasonal variability in temperature and salinity than the open ocean at similar latitudes. The residence time of water in the harbor is longer than in an open coastal area, which can allow for more of the CO2 uptake from OAE to occur locally, making monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) more manageable. However, this containment also limits the total rate at which alkalinity can be added without causing significant local changes in water chemistry.

In contrast, the open ocean has a much larger surface area and volume, leading to more stable temperature and salinity conditions and a higher buffering capacity (Revelle factor) on a large scale. The gas transfer velocity, heavily influenced by wind speed, is generally higher and more persistent in the open ocean than in a semi-enclosed harbor, which can lead to faster air-sea CO2 exchange. A global 1-degree ocean model, such as a hypothetical "Zhou 2024 atlas," would likely not resolve the complex, fine-scale dynamics of a specific coastal system like Halifax Harbour. Such models are designed to capture large-scale ocean circulation and biogeochemistry, but a 1-degree resolution (approximately 111 kilometers at the equator) is too coarse to accurately represent the unique estuarine circulation, stratification, and residence times of a harbor, which are critical for predicting the local impact and efficacy of an OAE project. Therefore, high-resolution nested models are necessary for such assessments. There remains uncertainty in precisely quantifying gas exchange and the full ecosystem response in such a dynamic coastal environment.

### Carbon Dioxide Removal in Marginal Seas (2024-2026): A Comprehensive Scan

A thorough review of Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR) activities between 2024 and 2026 in key marginal and semi-enclosed seas reveals a landscape dominated by sub-seabed geological storage of captured CO2 (a form of Carbon Capture and Storage, CCS), particularly in the North Sea and Gulf of Mexico. Marine CDR (mCDR) methods that remove CO2 directly from seawater are in nascent, research, and pilot stages, with only a few specific projects emerging. Many of the listed seas show no significant, publicly documented CDR deployments, though research interest is growing.

This analysis distinguishes between marketing claims and verified results by focusing on official project statements, research publications, and government permits.

### **CDR Deployments & Projects by Sea (2024-2026)**

#### **North Sea**
The North Sea is the global leader in operational and planned geological CO2 storage projects, leveraging its history of oil and gas extraction. These are primarily CCS projects, not mCDR from the water column.

*   **Project:** **Northern Lights**
    *   **Deployer/s:** Equinor, Shell, TotalEnergies.
    *   **Location:** Norwegian North Sea.
    *   **Mechanism:** Geological sequestration. CO2 is captured from industrial sources in Europe, shipped to a terminal in Øygarden, Norway, and injected into a sub-seabed reservoir for permanent storage.
    *   **Status & Verified Results:** Phase 1 is operational, with the first CO2 injected in August 2025, storing up to 1.5 million tonnes per year (Mtpa). Phase 2, aiming to increase capacity to 5 Mtpa, received a final investment decision in March 2025. This is a verified, operational project.

*   **Project:** **Greensand**
    *   **Deployer/s:** INEOS, Harbour Energy, Nordsøfonden.
    *   **Location:** Nini West oilfield, Danish North Sea.
    *   **Mechanism:** Geological sequestration. Liquefied CO2 from sources like Danish biogas facilities will be shipped from the Port of Esbjerg and injected into depleted oil reservoirs 1,800 meters beneath the seabed.
    *   **Status & Verified Results:** Denmark granted the first-ever CO2 storage permit in late 2025. Injection is scheduled for 2026, starting at 400,000 tons per year and aiming to scale to 8 Mtpa by 2030. Construction of onshore terminals and carrier vessels is underway. The project is permitted and moving to the operational phase.

*   **Project:** **Research into Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement (OAE)**
    *   **Deployer/s:** CDRmare research mission (involving various German research institutes).
    *   **Location:** German North Sea waters.
    *   **Mechanism:** Research into the feasibility of OAE by distributing alkaline solutions (silicate- or lime-based) or ground volcanic rock.
    *   **Status & Verified Results:** This is a research program, not a deployment. A 2025 feasibility assessment concluded that options are limited but identified OAE as potentially feasible. There are no verified large-scale results, and the focus is on assessing potential, limitations, and environmental impacts.

#### **Black Sea**
The Black Sea's unique anoxic (oxygen-free) deep waters present a novel opportunity for biomass-based CDR.

*   **Project:** **Project MACS Romania**
    *   **Deployer/s:** Rewind (an Israeli startup).
    *   **Location:** Constanta, Romania.
    *   **Mechanism:** Biomass Carbon Removal and Storage (BiCRS). The project plans to collect agricultural and forestry residues, transport them offshore, and sink them to the deep, anoxic floor of the Black Sea. The lack of oxygen is expected to prevent decomposition, permanently sequestering the carbon embodied in the biomass.
    *   **Status & Verified Results:** The project is in the pilot permitting and Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) phase. Rewind has conducted experiments showing high preservation rates of biomass in anoxic conditions. These are preliminary research results, not verified large-scale carbon removal. The company's claims of sequestering a gigaton of CO2 annually are marketing projections, not current reality.

An EPA permit was granted in April 2026 for a rare offshore biomass storage field trial in the Gulf. ### **Air-Water CO2 Equilibration in Marginal Seas vs. the Open Ocean**

Air-water CO2 equilibration in semi-enclosed seas differs fundamentally from the open ocean due to restricted water exchange, which leads to longer residence times and strong stratification. These basins are heavily influenced by terrestrial inputs and unique local conditions, creating a higher Revelle factor (lower CO2 buffering capacity) in some cases, like the low-alkalinity Baltic Sea, making them more sensitive to CO2 additions. Strong vertical stratification, driven by temperature and salinity gradients (e.g., freshwater input in the Baltic), acts as a lid, severely limiting the exchange between the surface layer where CO2 is absorbed and the vast deep-water volume where it could be stored, a process that is much more efficient in the well-mixed open ocean.

A global 1-degree ocean model, like a hypothetical "Zhou 2024 atlas," would fail to resolve these critical dynamics. Such coarse-resolution models cannot adequately represent the complex coastlines, shallow bathymetry, localized upwelling, and strong stratification that govern air-sea exchange and residence times in these marginal seas. This leads to significant uncertainties and likely erroneous estimations of CRT and carbon cycling, making such models unsuitable for accurately assessing CDR potential or impacts in these specific, highly heterogeneous environments without significant downscaling or specialized coastal components.

## Rising Tide of Innovation: A Global Scan of Coastal Carbon Dioxide Removal (2024-2026)

**A burgeoning industry of carbon dioxide removal (CDR) is turning its attention to the world's estuaries, deltas, fjords, bays, lagoons, and nearshore coastal zones. These dynamic environments are becoming testbeds for a range of technologies aiming to enhance the ocean's natural ability to absorb and store atmospheric CO2. ### Corporate and Project Developments in Coastal CDR

A growing number of companies are piloting and deploying CDR technologies in coastal waters. These efforts primarily fall into three categories: Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement (OAE), Direct Ocean Capture (DOC), and nature-based solutions like seaweed cultivation.

#### Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement (OAE)

OAE involves adding alkaline substances to seawater to enhance its capacity to absorb and store CO2.

*   **Vesta:** This public benefit corporation is a prominent player in coastal enhanced weathering (CEW), a subset of OAE.
    *   **Mechanism:** Vesta grinds olivine, a volcanic mineral, into sand and deploys it in coastal areas. The wave action accelerates the dissolution of olivine, which increases the alkalinity of the seawater, enabling it to draw down more CO2 from the atmosphere and store it as bicarbonate. The company claims this process also helps in de-acidifying the ocean.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Peconic Bay, Southampton, New York, USA:** In July 2022, Vesta placed 650 tonnes of olivine sand along an intertidal shoreline as part of a beach nourishment project. This was the world's first marine Enhanced Rock Weathering field trial. Data collection to assess the efficacy and environmental impacts concluded in November 2023, with analysis currently underway by Hourglass and other scientific collaborators.
        *   **Duck, North Carolina, USA:** An upcoming project is planned.
        *   **Caribbean:** Pilot beaches are currently in operation.
        *   **Massachusetts, USA:** A pilot experiment in a saltmarsh is being developed in collaboration with Woods Hole Oceanographic Institute, the National Park Service, and the US Geological Survey.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** Vesta cites over 30 years of lab-based research supporting the potential of this method. Preliminary results from their New York field trial after one year showed no adverse changes to in-field chlorophyll A concentrations. A laboratory study also indicated no negative impacts on various phytoplankton groups even at high concentrations of olivine constituents. However, comprehensive, peer-reviewed results from their field trials demonstrating the net carbon removal and long-term ecological impacts are still in progress. Vesta's claim of potentially removing over a gigatonne of CO2 annually at scale is a forward-looking projection.

*   **Planetary Technologies:** This Canadian company is also focused on OAE through a process that purifies alkaline rocks from mining waste.
    *   **Mechanism:** The company's Accelerated Carbon Transition (ACT) platform purifies alkaline rocks left over from mining. This purified alkalinity is then added to the ocean to enhance its carbon sequestration capacity. A by-product of this process is green hydrogen.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Quebec and Nova Scotia, Canada:** Pilot plants were scheduled to come online in phases starting in late 2022 to demonstrate a scaled version of their process. These pilots are integrated with ocean research projects to monitor effects.
        *   **Gatineau, Quebec, Canada:** Listed as a location for the company.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** Planetary has pre-sold carbon credits to companies like Shopify based on the projected removal from its pilot plants. The company is still in the testing phase to ensure safety and determine the appropriate input limits for various ocean locations. While the technology is backed by scientific principles, large-scale, verified carbon removal and long-term environmental safety data from their field deployments are pending.

*   **Running Tide:** This company has explored multiple CDR pathways, including OAE and biomass sinking.
    *   **Mechanism:** Running Tide's operations have involved sinking terrestrial biomass (wood chips) coated with alkaline material (lime-kiln dust) in the ocean. This combines biomass sinking with ocean alkalinity enhancement.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Akranes and Grundartangi, Iceland:** Operations are based in these coastal communities. In 2023, the company conducted 15 research deployments, sinking alkaline-coated wood at the edge of Iceland's exclusive economic zone.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** Running Tide announced in March 2024 that it had delivered over 21,000 tonnes of carbon removal to customers like Microsoft and Shopify. However, the company faced scrutiny over its methods and the scientific basis for its carbon removal claims. Critics pointed out the lack of baseline data and robust modeling to verify the claimed carbon sequestration. In June 2024, Running Tide announced it was shutting down its global operations due to a lack of financing. An independent science advisory board, facilitated by Ocean Visions, had been established to provide guidance, but the company closed before many of its recommendations for further research could be implemented.

#### Direct Ocean Capture (DOC)

DOC technologies directly extract dissolved CO2 from seawater, allowing the ocean to absorb more from the atmosphere.

*   **Captura:** This California-based company has developed a DOC system that is being validated for commercial use.
    *   **Mechanism:** Captura's technology uses a proprietary electrodialysis process powered by renewable energy to separate a small stream of seawater into an acid and a base. The acid is used to release dissolved CO2 from the bulk seawater, which is then captured. The base is then used to neutralize the water before it is returned to the ocean.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Port of Los Angeles, California, USA:** A pilot plant with a capacity of 100 tons of CO2 per year is in operation.
        *   **Kona, Hawaii, USA:** A larger pilot plant with a 1,000-ton-per-year capacity, developed in partnership with Equinor, began operations in February 2025. This facility is located at the Hawaii Ocean Science and Technology (HOST) Park.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** In November 2025, Captura and Equinor announced the successful completion of a joint technology qualification program at the Kona facility, validating the system for commercial deployment based on 20 key performance metrics. The company is now planning its first commercial-scale facility. While this validation is a significant step, detailed, independently verified performance data and long-term environmental monitoring results are still forthcoming. Preliminary modeling suggests an oceanic uptake of atmospheric CO2 in the range of 65-98% of the direct ocean capture extraction rate.

*   **Equatic:** A spin-off from UCLA, Equatic's technology removes CO2 from seawater while also producing green hydrogen.
    *   **Mechanism:** The process uses electrolysis to pass an electrical current through seawater, which accelerates the natural process of CO2 removal and traps it in solid calcium and magnesium carbonates. This process also produces hydrogen as a co-product.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Port of Los Angeles, California, USA:** A pilot plant has been operational since March 2023.
        *   **Singapore:** A pilot plant was previously in operation at the Tuas Desalination Plant. The company is now constructing a larger demonstration plant in Singapore that broke ground in 2024.
        *   **Canada:** A commercial-scale plant is planned.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** Equatic has successfully operated two pilot plants and is now moving to a larger demonstration scale. The company has secured contracts for over 75,000 tonnes of CO2 removal. The technology's dual-product output (carbon removal and hydrogen) is a key part of its commercialization strategy. As with other emerging CDR technologies, long-term, independently verified data on carbon removal efficiency and environmental impact at scale are needed.

*   **Ebb Carbon:** This company is developing an electrochemical method to enhance ocean alkalinity.
    *   **Mechanism:** Ebb Carbon's system uses an electrochemical process to separate seawater into an acidic and an alkaline stream. The alkaline stream is returned to the ocean to increase its capacity to absorb atmospheric CO2.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Sequim Bay, Washington, USA:** A demonstration-scale unit, capable of sequestering 100 tons of CO2 per year, was deployed in November 2023 at the Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL).
        *   **Port Angeles, Washington, USA:** "Project Macoma," a temporary pilot project, began operations in October 2025 at the Port of Port Angeles on the Strait of Juan de Fuca. This project aims to remove 1,000 tons of CO2 over its two-year operation and has secured the first-of-its-kind National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES) permit for an OAE pilot.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** Ebb Carbon's work at PNNL has provided real-world validation that the system operates safely and within existing regulatory frameworks. The company emphasizes its collaborative approach with scientists and the local community to build trust and ensure responsible development. The launch of Project Macoma under a formal permit is a significant step in moving from research to regulated commercial activity. However, the net carbon removal and ecological impacts will be assessed over the course of the pilot.

#### Nature-Based Solutions

These approaches leverage natural biological processes, such as photosynthesis in seaweed, to capture carbon.

*   **Coastal Carbon:** This company is focused on using AI and satellite imagery to monitor seaweed biomass.
    *   **Mechanism:** While not directly a CDR company, Coastal Carbon provides the critical monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) technology needed for seaweed-based CDR projects. Their AI models analyze satellite data to measure seaweed biomass, which is essential for quantifying carbon stored in these ecosystems.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **British Columbia, Canada:** The company has partnered with the Coastal First Nations Great Bear Initiative and Ocean Wise to pilot their remote monitoring solution along the British Columbia coastline.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** Coastal Carbon is developing the tools to enable verified carbon accounting for seaweed restoration and expansion projects. Their success will be measured by the accuracy and scalability of their monitoring technology, which is still in the pilot phase.

*   **SINTEF:** This Norwegian research organization is developing technology for large-scale kelp cultivation for CDR.
    *   **Mechanism:** SINTEF Ocean is working on scalable technology for seaweed CDR, including identifying offshore cultivation sites, developing cultivation strategies, and studying the CDR effect of sinking kelp biomass.
    *   **Locations:**
        *   **Norwegian Sea:** Research is focused on high-productive areas in the Norwegian Sea.
    *   **Verified Results vs. Marketing Claims:** SINTEF's work is in the research and development phase. They project a high potential for carbon sequestration through seaweed farming in the Norwegian Sea, but acknowledge that the actual potential needs quantification and the technology for long-term carbon storage needs to be developed and scaled.

### Air-Water CO2 Equilibration: Coastal vs. Open Ocean

Air-water CO2 equilibration in estuaries, deltas, fjords, and other nearshore zones is fundamentally different and more complex than in the open ocean, a reality that global 1-degree ocean models, such as the one described in the "Zhou 2024 atlas," would struggle to resolve. These coastal systems exhibit much higher spatial and temporal variability in their carbonate chemistry due to a confluence of factors including riverine inputs, high biological productivity, and restricted exchange with the open ocean. For instance, estuaries often have a higher Revelle factor—a measure of the ocean's buffering capacity—meaning they can absorb less CO2 for a given increase in atmospheric CO2 compared to the open ocean. Furthermore, strong stratification from freshwater runoff in fjords and deltas can limit the vertical mixing of CO2, while shallow depths and variable wind patterns create complex gas transfer dynamics that are not captured by coarse-resolution models. A global 1-degree ocean model lacks the fine-scale resolution to represent the intricate bathymetry, circulation patterns, and sharp biogeochemical gradients characteristic of these coastal environments, leading to significant uncertainties in their role in the global carbon cycle.

A project at Northwestern University, funded by the Department of Energy and starting in 2025, will test the efficacy of enhanced weathering using crushed limestone on an Illinois farm, which could have implications for understanding carbon removal in the broader Midwest.
*   **Indigenous-led Projects:** The Fond du Lac Band of Lake Superior Chippewa and the Keweenaw Bay Indian Community of Lake Superior Chippewa have approved forest-based carbon sequestration projects. These projects, while not directly in the lakes, are significant regional carbon removal efforts.

### Air-Water CO2 Equilibration: Freshwater Lakes vs. The Open Ocean

Air-water CO2 equilibration in large freshwater lakes like the Great Lakes differs significantly from the open ocean due to their distinct carbonate chemistry, lower buffering capacity, and unique physical dynamics. Freshwater systems have a higher and more variable Revelle factor, a measure of the resistance to absorbing atmospheric CO2, because of their lower alkalinity and dissolved inorganic carbon concentrations. This means that for a given increase in atmospheric CO2, the change in dissolved CO2 in freshwater is larger, leading to a more rapid increase in acidity and a more limited long-term storage capacity compared to the ocean. Additionally, factors like shorter surface residence times, seasonal stratification that can lead to CO2 buildup in deeper waters, and gas transfer velocities that are highly dependent on lake size and wind speed create a more dynamic and heterogeneous environment for CO2 exchange than the more buffered and vast open ocean.

A global 1-degree ocean model, such as the one referenced by Zhou (2024), would likely not resolve these critical differences with sufficient accuracy. Such models are designed for the chemical and physical properties of saltwater and their coarse resolution would fail to capture the significant spatial and temporal variability in pH, alkalinity, and stratification that characterize large lakes. The complex shoreline interactions, riverine inputs, and more rapid response to atmospheric changes in freshwater systems necessitate higher-resolution, specialized models that can account for their unique biogeochemistry. There is considerable uncertainty in accurately modeling these systems, and applying a global ocean model would likely lead to significant biases in estimating CO2 fluxes and the potential efficacy of CDR strategies in the Great Lakes.

### **CO2 Equilibration: In-River Outgassing vs. Downstream at Sea**

A critical uncertainty in riverine enhanced weathering is the fate of the newly formed bicarbonate. The process is only effective for long-term carbon removal if the bicarbonate is transported to the ocean, where it has a residence time of over 10,000 years. However, changes in water chemistry can lead to the re-release of CO2 (outgassing) within the river itself, negating the climate benefit.

Modeling studies show that if rivers are at equilibrium with respect to calcite, approximately two-thirds of the carbon dissolved in soil waters could be lost through precipitation in rivers, releasing CO2 back into the atmosphere. This suggests that the capacity of rivers to transport the products of enhanced weathering is a key limiting factor. One study on UK rivers predicted that secondary carbonate precipitation could make enhanced weathering 16-27% less effective at removing CO2 than initially hoped. However, a dynamic river network model for North American watersheds predicted low carbon loss (<5%) for many river pathways, but also noted that significantly higher degassing (>15%) can occur, highlighting the need for site-specific assessments.

### **Air-Water CO2 Equilibration: Rivers vs. The Open Ocean**

The processes governing the exchange of CO2 between the water surface and the atmosphere differ significantly between rivers and the open ocean.

*   **Buffering/Revelle Factor:** The Revelle factor measures the resistance of surface water to absorbing atmospheric CO2. In the open ocean, this factor is typically between 8 and 13. As the ocean absorbs more CO2, the Revelle factor increases, reducing its buffering capacity. Freshwater systems, like rivers, generally have lower alkalinity and a different chemical composition than seawater, which affects their buffering capacity. The addition of alkalinity through enhanced weathering is designed to counteract this and increase the capacity to hold dissolved inorganic carbon.

*   **Surface Residence Time:** Rivers have a much shorter water residence time compared to the vast open ocean. This means there is less time for the water to equilibrate with the atmosphere before it reaches the sea. The equilibration of CO2 in the surface ocean with the atmosphere can take about a year. The rapid flow of rivers can thus help transport the dissolved bicarbonate to the ocean before significant outgassing occurs, although this is highly dependent on river characteristics.

*   **Gas-Transfer/Wind:** Gas transfer velocity is influenced by factors like wind speed and water turbulence. While wind is a dominant factor in the open ocean, the turbulence created by river flow over varied riverbeds can also significantly enhance gas exchange.

*   **Stratification:** In both rivers and the ocean, stratification (the layering of water due to density differences) can act as a barrier to vertical mixing. In some cases, this can inhibit CO2 from deeper, CO2-rich water from reaching the surface and outgassing. In estuaries where freshwater from rivers meets denser seawater, stratification is a prominent feature that can influence mixing and residence times.

### **Resolution in Global Ocean Models**

A global 1-degree ocean model, such as the conceptual "Zhou 2024 atlas" (noting the actual Zhou et al. 2024 paper is on tidal channel formation), would likely not resolve the fine-scale processes critical to riverine enhanced weathering. Global ocean biogeochemical models are essential for understanding large-scale carbon cycling but have known limitations in coastal and riverine zones. These models often have coarse resolutions (a 1-degree grid is roughly 111 km by 111 km at the equator) and may not accurately represent the complex hydrodynamics, biogeochemistry, and sharp gradients found in river plumes and estuaries. Research indicates that while implementing riverine inputs improves model skill in coastal regions, the response in the open ocean can be overestimated due to how these inputs are advected offshore. Therefore, accurately predicting the fate of alkalinity and carbon from a specific river would require higher-resolution regional models that can better simulate nearshore processes.

This approach leverages existing wastewater treatment plants (WWTPs) as infrastructure for carbon dioxide removal (CDR). Air-water CO2 equilibration in the coastal and estuarine waters where these facilities discharge is fundamentally different from the open ocean. ### **Defining 'Marine' vs. 'Aquatic' CDR**

The terminology in the CDR space is evolving, but a general distinction can be made:

*   **Marine CDR (mCDR):** This term is consistently used to refer to CDR approaches that take place in or leverage the ocean and its ecosystems. It is broadly divided into two categories:
    *   **Abiotic (or chemical) mCDR:** These methods alter seawater chemistry to enhance CO2 uptake. The most prominent example is Ocean Alkalinity Enhancement (OAE), which involves adding alkaline minerals to the ocean. Direct Ocean Capture (DOC), which electrochemically removes CO2 from seawater, also falls into this category.
    *   **Biotic (or biological) mCDR:** These approaches rely on marine life. Examples include cultivating and sinking macroalgae (like kelp) and restoring coastal "blue carbon" ecosystems such as mangroves and seagrass beds.
*   **Aquatic CDR:** This is a broader, less formally defined term that can encompass any water-based CDR, including marine, freshwater (rivers, lakes), and engineered water systems (groundwater, mine water, industrial water). While "marine" specifically denotes the saltwater ocean environment, "aquatic" is more general. Carbon removal registries and practitioners tend to use "marine CDR" or "ocean-based CDR" for ocean methods and are developing specific methodologies for terrestrial approaches like enhanced weathering that have an aquatic component (i.e., bicarbonate runoff).

### **Air-Water CO2 Equilibration: Water Bodies vs. the Open Ocean**

The process of CO2 moving between the atmosphere and a water body differs significantly between the vast open ocean and the smaller, more varied water bodies discussed above.

*   **Buffering/Revelle Factor:** The open ocean has a massive volume and a carbonate chemistry system that buffers against rapid changes in pH, a phenomenon described by the Revelle factor. While this slows the ocean's uptake of atmospheric CO2, it also provides immense storage capacity. In contrast, smaller water bodies like rivers, lakes, and aquifers have much lower volumes and can have highly variable chemistry. Their buffering capacity is localized and can be quickly overwhelmed by alkalinity additions or acidic inputs, leading to more dramatic but less stable shifts in CO2 uptake.
*   **Surface Residence and Gas Transfer:** The open ocean's surface layer is vast, and water can remain in contact with the atmosphere for months to years, allowing for gradual equilibration. Gas transfer is heavily influenced by wind speed over large areas. In rivers, water is in constant, turbulent motion, leading to rapid gas exchange, but the residence time at the surface is very short before the water moves downstream. In aquifers, the "surface" is the interface with soil gases, which have much higher CO2 concentrations than the atmosphere, and water movement is extremely slow, leading to very different equilibration dynamics. Stratified lakes and mine pit lakes may have limited mixing between surface and deep waters, restricting the transport of absorbed CO2 to depth.
*   **Stratification:** The open ocean exhibits large-scale, persistent thermal stratification that separates the surface from the deep ocean, playing a key role in long-term carbon sequestration. Rivers are generally well-mixed, while lakes and mine pits can be strongly stratified in summer and mix in winter. This stratification can limit the effectiveness of CDR by preventing CO2-depleted surface water from mixing and allowing for further atmospheric uptake.

**Applicability of a Global 1-Degree Ocean Model (e.g., Zhou 2024):** A global 1-degree ocean model, which has a resolution of approximately 111 kilometers at the equator, would be entirely unsuitable for resolving the CDR processes in these non-oceanic water bodies. Such models are designed to simulate large-scale ocean circulation, temperature, and biogeochemistry. They cannot resolve the fine-scale features of rivers, lakes, mine pits, or groundwater systems. Modeling CDR in these environments requires highly specialized, local-scale hydrological and geochemical models that can account for the specific geometry, chemistry, and flow dynamics of the water body in question, with significant uncertainty remaining even with such tools.
